IS MY DATA STILL THERE?

Every storage medium has a lifespan and yours might have already reached its end. Whether it is a box of burned CDs in a closet or a hard drive stored in a library from 2008, that data may no longer be accessible.

01 / Storage types

How data is stored and what degrades it

There are many types of data storage available, but not all storage is created equal. Older storage mediums like ones you saved on in 1999, 2005, or even 2015 were all developed and used depending on what was affordable and available at the time, not necessarily by what would last the longest.

Here is a breakdown of the four main types of data storage and what happens to your files over time.

Click each type to learn more.

OPTICAL

Examples: CDs, DVDs, Blu-ray

Lifespan: 30–100 years, which varies by disc type

ROM/R — Up to 100 years

RW — Up to 30 years

Each disc surface contains microscopic light and dark spots that are read by a laser in a disc drive. There are different types of discs: factory-pressed ROM (read-only memory) discs, CD-R and DVD-R discs you could burn on your own but not edit once burned, and CD-RWs that you could burn at home and re-write as needed.

All optical media storage types are sensitive to scratches, humidity, and UV light. For example, the optimal 100 year lifespan listed above for ROM discs only applies to those kept in ideal storage conditions: climate controlled environments that are protected from physical damage and UV light exposure.

MAGNETIC

Examples: Hard drives, floppy disks, VHS tapes, cassette tapes

Lifespan: 3–30 years

Magnetic storage works through data represented by microscopic magnetized particles on a disk or tape surface. Hard disk drives use spinning platters with read-write heads that hover just above the surface. If that head makes contact with the platter, data can be permanently lost.

Experts estimate the lifespan of magnetic media at around three years, recommending you recopy data every two years to keep it safe.

Magnetic storage is also vulnerable to heat, dust, mold, and magnetic fields. Additionally, the hardware needed to read older magnetic formats like floppy disks is almost completely gone from existence.

FLASH / SOLID STATE

Examples: USB drives, SSDs, SD cards

Lifespan: 5–10 years

Flash or solid state storage holds data in electronic cells with no moving parts, making it physically durable compared to something like a VHS or cassette tape, but even these types of storage devices will not last indefinitely.

Flash storage devices left unpowered over time lose their charge, potentially causing data corruption even without physical damage. They are also vulnerable to dust, debris, and physical elements clogging their ports if not properly covered and stored.

CLOUD / NETWORK

Examples: Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox

Lifespan: Ongoing*

Cloud storage is a complete shift from physical storage. With cloud storage, you entrust your data to an outside company's servers rather than storing it on a physical device you own. This outside provider's infrastructure is the only real risk to your data over time, meaning that access to your data depends entirely on that company's service remaining active and your account staying in good standing.

This is currently the most reliable option for long term and ongoing data preservation, but it relies heavily on variables mostly out of your control. As a failsafe, many cloud services suggest hard backups, which can seem redundant.

*Only as long as the service exists and your account remains active.

02 / Format obsolescence

Disc survival is only half the battle

You have a mint condition floppy disk from 1989 that you stored in a climate-controlled dark room, but unfortunately it is still likely unreadable today. Why? Because the hardware and software needed to read it has largely disappeared. This is called format obsolescence: when the technology required to access data becomes unavailable before the data itself is lost. Sure, you could buy a disk reader and an old computer from eBay, but even that is becoming harder to do because each year there are fewer working examples of this hardware and scarcity drives prices. Is your 1989 mint copy of "Leisure Suit Larry III" really worth a $6,000 price tag on the drive needed to play it?

Libraries and archives face this challenge constantly. A public library that saved scores of scans of local historic newspapers onto CD-RWs in the 1990s may now be dealing with degradation, format obsolescence, and a minimal supply of optical drives. The same is true for people at home with tons of digital photos saved on Zip disks, MiniDVs, or even early digital camera SD memory cards.

Format obsolescence timeline

Selected storage formats and their likely usability lifespans.

1970s

8-inch floppy disk

Effectively unreadable today without specialized hardware. Drives are rare and expensive while surviving disks are brittle and at risk for breaking.

1980s

5.25-inch floppy disk, Betamax, VHS

5.25-inch floppy disks are largely unreadable without specialized hardware. VHS and Betamax tapes are degrading rapidly while their playback hardware is also becoming scarce and expensive to source.

1990s

3.5-inch floppy disk, Zip disk, early CD-R

3.5-inch disk drives have been removed from modern computers and are becoming scarce and expensive to source. Zip drives are nearly extinct. CD-Rs from this era are at high risk of dye degradation now, and CD drives are also becoming scarce and expensive to source.

2000s

DVD-R, MiniDV, HD DVD, early flash drives

Home-burned DVD-Rs are approaching the limits of their expected lifespan. MiniDV camcorders are rare, while HD DVDs are already obsolete. Early flash drives from this era are also at risk especially if left unpowered.

2010s

Optical drives, older USB-A drives, early SSDs

Optical drives are only a requested, expensive and almost extinct option for computers and laptops at this point. External drives are inconsistent in performance. Flash drives from this era may have charge loss issues if they have been left unpowered for years. Early SSDs from this era were a big improvement in speed over hard drives, but they are increasingly unreliable for long-term storage.

Now

The time to act is now

Current best practice for viable storage devices: back them up in cloud storage combined with external SSD and periodic new formats. No single solution is all-encompassing.

⚠ Important — Don't wait!

There is no dramatic process involved with data loss. You have likely already experienced it. If you still have a boombox with a CD player or a CD player in your car, try one of your burned "mixtape" CD-RWs from your best friend in middle school. You know the ones. There is a very good chance that nothing will read or, if you are really lucky and that CD case in your closet was a fantastic storage spot, you might have access to a few snippets. Likely though, it will not play and an "unreadable" message might display.

You also see it with flash storage and you likely already have. A flash drive that worked fine in 2010 returns "unreadable" or "corrupted data" errors in 2026. By the time the problem is obvious, recovery is often expensive or impossible.

The window to act to back up your data in a different format is now, while your data is still accessible and the hardware needed to read it still exists.

04 / What should I do?

The 3-2-1 rule for data preservation

Information professionals and archivists frequently use the 3-2-1 backup rule as a basic standard for data safety. It is simple, effective, and useful for everyone concerned about data longevity to follow:

3

Keep 3 copies

Of any data you cannot afford to lose. One copy is not a reliable backup.

2

On 2 different types of storage

Instead of keeping all copies on the same type of storage. If they share a storage medium, they share the same risks of degradation or failure.

1

Store offsite

At least one copy of your data. This could be cloud storage, a relative's home, or a different, secure location.

For physical media you already own (e.g. old CDs, VHS tapes, external hard drives, etc.), the most urgent step is to transfer the data now, while the data is still able to be read by extant hardware. Many public libraries offer help with digitization, accessing older media, or pointing you to local resources for assistance.

05 / About this site

Why I built this

This site was created as a webpage project for LIS 5268: Technology for Information Professionals at the University of South Florida's School of Information, where I am currently pursuing my Master of Library and Information Science.

The idea for this subject matter came from my work as a Library Services Assistant at Hernando County Public Library, where questions about older data storage formats and digitization come up more frequently than you'd think. When a colleague mentioned excitedly that VHS tapes might be making a comeback, I realized that format obsolescence is something everyone, especially information professionals, needs to understand. It's also something the general public rarely thinks about until it's too late.

About the design

I can assure you that the Y2K-ish aesthetic is intentionally nostalgic. An information page about data storage obsolescence that looks like it's straight out of the era of burnt CDs full of Limewire MP3s seemed like a good design choice. The font switch buttons and the note about using the browser's zoom feature reflect my commitment to accessibility.
